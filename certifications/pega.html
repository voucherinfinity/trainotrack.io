ISACA Certifications Levels Explained

ISACA certifications are not organized by levels such as foundational, associate, or professional, but they are aligned to specific professional roles and domains. Each one is designed for a certain area of expertise and is best for those who work in different phases of their IT or cybersecurity careers.

  • CISA (Certified Information Systems Auditor) is perfect for IT auditors and assurance professionals who focus on system controls and compliance issues.
  • CISM (Certified Information Security Manager) is aimed at information security managers and thus, it provides them with the means for building and managing enterprise security strategies.
  • CRISC (Certified in Risk and Information Systems Control) is primarily for enterprise risk and control personnel who pinpoint and manage the IT and business risks.
  • CGEIT (Certified in the Governance of Enterprise IT) is for those professionals who supervise the IT governance and the alignment of IT strategies with business objectives.

Generally, the ISACA certifications are widely recognized throughout the world, are vendor-neutral, and aimed at providing professionals with practical and leadership-level knowledge, depending on the stage of their careers they are in.

How to Prepare and Get Certified

ISACA certifications stand for the utmost quality, and one has to have not only theoretical knowledge but also practical insight in order to succeed in them. The first step is to identify a suitable certification that corresponds to your job or career ambition. After the selection, candidates need to go through the official ISACA exam guide, which describes the covered domains, the weightage of each topic, and key knowledge areas.

ISACA sells official study materials, review manuals, and question banks electronically. Besides this, various online training providers like Simplilearn, Infosec, and Udemy launch updated courses and mock tests for each ISACA certification. Joining a study group or an ISACA chapter can also support you in continuing your studies and benefiting from colleagues who are preparing for the same exam.

Practice is the best teacher. A majority of ISACA exams mainly test the practical application of concepts, so professionals working in audit, security, governance, or risk sectors find the material quite sensible. ISACA further determines the experience requirements for most certificationsâ€”generally 3 to 5 years of service in a related fieldâ€”which are to be fulfilled after passing the exam if one wants to receive the certificate.

To register for an exam, applicants need to submit their applications on the MyISACA portal, pay the exam fee, and pick a time slot on the schedule that matches their time zone through PSI, ISACAâ€™s exam partner. Depending on the mode of examination, one can take the exams remotely or at designated centers.
